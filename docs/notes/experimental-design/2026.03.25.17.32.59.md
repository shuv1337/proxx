You get hidden scalability pain when the **router’s decision path becomes a chatty, cross-cutting tangle of strategies and policy calls**, especially under multi-tenant load; production systems tend to refactor by separating “fast, local, cacheable routing logic” from “slower, global policy decisions,” tightening boundaries and aggressively simplifying the decision graph. [styra](https://www.styra.com/blog/opa-management-challenges-and-opportunities/)

## 1. Over‑engineered routing logic

Routing systems that try to simultaneously optimize for cost, latency, safety, capability, and provider health often end up with deeply nested strategies and policy checks for every request, which adds hundreds of milliseconds of overhead at scale. Guidance from multi‑LLM routing in high‑volume systems explicitly warns that complex, over‑engineered routing often underperforms simple rules and introduces avoidable latency. [mindstudio](https://www.mindstudio.ai/blog/ai-agent-builder-multi-llm-flexibility/)

**Refactor pattern:**

- Introduce a tiered decision path: cheap, deterministic rules first (e.g., “simple Q&A → cheap model”), then fall back to more expensive analysis only for ambiguous or high‑value requests. [portkey](https://portkey.ai/blog/llm-routing-techniques-for-high-volume-applications)
- Separate “routing policy” (which models are even allowed for this tenant/region/use case) from “routing strategy” (which one is best right now), so you can keep strategies fast and mostly in-process while policies change more slowly. [docs.aws.amazon](https://docs.aws.amazon.com/prescriptive-guidance/latest/saas-multitenant-api-access-authorization/introduction.html)

## 2. Policy engine as a synchronous choke point

If every router call synchronously asks an external PDP/OPA‑like service for a decision, that PDP becomes both a latency bottleneck and a scaling hotspot. Experience reports around OPA and similar engines highlight management overhead, the cost of JSON encode/decode, and the difficulty of distributing policies and data across many instances without building a central control plane. [reddit](https://www.reddit.com/r/microservices/comments/1h5sezs/seeking_advice_on_implementing_dynamic/)

**Refactor pattern:**

- Co‑locate policy evaluation with the router (same process or sidecar) and keep it **data‑light**, using an event‑driven sync system (e.g., OPAL‑style) to stream policy/data updates instead of fetching on every request. [reddit](https://www.reddit.com/r/microservices/comments/1h5sezs/seeking_advice_on_implementing_dynamic/)
- Cache policy decisions or intermediate facts for short windows where correctness permits, so only a fraction of requests incur full policy evaluation cost; keep strict time bounds on staleness. [permit](https://www.permit.io/blog/policy-engine-showdown-opa-vs-openfga-vs-cedar)

## 3. Strategy explosion and configuration combinatorics

As you add providers, model families, tenants, and traffic classes, a straightforward Strategy implementation can devolve into dozens of specialized strategies (per‑tenant, per‑region, per‑capability), each wired to slightly different policy queries. Multi‑LLM routing research notes that joint modeling across many LLMs increases latency and complexity without always improving accuracy, which mirrors what happens when your strategy layer becomes a combinatorial grid. [arxiv](https://arxiv.org/html/2405.00467v1)

**Refactor pattern:**

- Collapse many fine‑grained strategies into a **small set of generic strategies** parameterized by configuration (e.g., “greedy best,” “cost‑first,” “safety‑first”) rather than code branches per combination. [aclanthology](https://aclanthology.org/2024.insights-1.15.pdf)
- Introduce a central “routing plan” concept: policies and tenant config define a plan (parameters and constraints), and strategies just execute plans, which keeps the code surface small while letting config handle variety.

## 4. Data‑heavy policies that don’t scale

When policy engines are used not just for authorization but also for routing and entitlements, teams often dump large blobs of contextual data into the policy engine so policies can “see everything.” Policy engine comparisons and OPA experience reports warn that complex policies plus megabytes of JSON data significantly hurt evaluation performance, and that engines like OPA are not designed to be general‑purpose, frequently updated data stores. [cerbos](https://www.cerbos.dev/blog/cerbos-vs-opa)

**Refactor pattern:**

- Shift from **data‑heavy policies to data‑light predicates**: policies reference a small set of attributes (tenant plan, region, risk level, feature flags), while the bulk of routing data (latency metrics, costs, provider SLOs) stays in the router’s own caches or metrics store. [permit](https://www.permit.io/blog/policy-engine-showdown-opa-vs-openfga-vs-cedar)
- Make policy decisions more declarative (“this tenant may use model class X in region Y”) and let the strategy layer choose specific instances using its own fast, ephemeral data.

## 5. Multi‑tenant and regional concerns hidden in rules

A subtle but nasty pitfall is encoding tenant isolation and regional placement entirely as policy rules sprinkled across strategies and policies, instead of having an explicit tenant/region execution context and clear PDP/PEP roles. Multi‑tenant SaaS guidance emphasizes modeling tenants explicitly, propagating tenant context everywhere, and designing clear PDP (decision) and PEP (enforcement) components for authorization and access control. [docs.aws.amazon](https://docs.aws.amazon.com/prescriptive-guidance/latest/saas-multitenant-api-access-authorization/design-models.html)

**Refactor pattern:**

- Define a first‑class **TenantContext** (tenant id, region, plan, data residency, compliance posture) that is resolved once at the edge and carried through strategies and policy evaluation. [workos](https://workos.com/blog/developers-guide-saas-multi-tenant-architecture)
- Give policies ownership of “what this tenant is allowed to do” and give strategies ownership of “how to route within what is allowed,” which keeps cross‑tenant and cross‑region rules from leaking into arbitrary code paths. [docs.aws.amazon](https://docs.aws.amazon.com/prescriptive-guidance/latest/saas-multitenant-api-access-authorization/introduction.html)

## 6. Opaque, hard‑to‑debug decisions

Combining a Strategy pattern with a separate policy engine can make routing behavior extremely hard to reason about: a single request might hit multiple strategies, each making one or more policy calls, with little centralized logging. OPA management write‑ups call out the lack of built‑in monitoring, policy testability, and decision logging as challenges for enterprise‑grade usage. [styra](https://www.styra.com/blog/opa-management-challenges-and-opportunities/)

**Refactor pattern:**

- Introduce a **decision log** schema: every routed request yields a single structured record capturing inputs, selected strategy, policy version, key policy decisions, and final model choice, which can then be queried or replayed. [portkey](https://portkey.ai/blog/llm-routing-techniques-for-high-volume-applications)
- Build a replay harness that can run historic traffic through new strategies/policies in shadow mode, a practice used in LLM routing research to compare routers and highlight fragility and suboptimal decisions before promoting changes. [arxiv](https://arxiv.org/html/2504.07113v1)

## 7. Learned routers and policy engines fighting each other

Once you add learned, preference‑based routers or classifiers on top of your strategy layer, you can end up with a “router inside the router”: ML models trying to choose the best LLM, wrapped in strategies, further gated by policies. Studies of router‑LLMs and learned routing show that such systems can be fragile, biased toward overusing the strongest model, and sensitive to training data and distribution shift, while also adding latency and complexity. [arxiv](https://arxiv.org/html/2504.07113v1)

**Refactor pattern:**

- Treat learned routers as **advisors, not authorities**: strategies can take router suggestions but still apply simple, robust heuristics (e.g., guardrails for safety, caps on high‑cost models, fallback models on low confidence). [arxiv](https://arxiv.org/html/2405.00467v1)
- Segment where ML routing is used (e.g., only for complex, ambiguous queries) and use static or simple rule‑based routing for the bulk traffic, a pattern recommended in high‑volume routing guides to avoid over‑engineering. [mindstudio](https://www.mindstudio.ai/blog/ai-agent-builder-multi-llm-flexibility/)

## 8. Cost and outage handling bolted on too late

Many MVP routers assume stable pricing and healthy providers, but at volume the real pain is cost drift and provider degradations. LLM routing guides for high‑volume systems explicitly point out cost instability at scale and the need to quickly shift traffic when models slow down or error rates spike. [portkey](https://portkey.ai/blog/llm-routing-techniques-for-high-volume-applications)

**Refactor pattern:**

- Split your architecture into a **control plane vs. data plane**:  
  - Control plane: policy management, provider catalog, pricing, SLO targets, AB experiments.  
  - Data plane (router): fast, tenant‑aware execution, with strategies that consult local health/cost metrics and circuit breakers instead of remote policies for every micro‑adjustment. [portkey](https://portkey.ai/blog/llm-routing-techniques-for-high-volume-applications)
- Keep cost/safety constraints in relatively stable policies, but make “current best choice” depend on dynamic metrics and SLOs maintained locally and refreshed asynchronously.
